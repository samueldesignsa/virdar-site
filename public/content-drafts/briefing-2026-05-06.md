## One Insight

Boutique fitness is a better walk-in vertical than it looks on paper.

The obvious objection: these are small studios with thin margins. They're not buying $18,000 systems. But the math works differently in fitness than in other verticals. A Pilates studio owner with 300 members at $149/month has a $44,700/month top line. A 5% monthly churn rate means $2,235/month walking out. The retention math is immediate and personal — these owners feel every cancellation because they often know the member by name. They're not skeptical about whether churn is a problem. They already believe it. The question is whether they believe there's a system that actually solves it.

The objection you'll hear is "I don't have the budget right now." This is almost always true for small studios. The response isn't to push harder — it's to make the math unavoidable. "What does it cost you per month when a member cancels?" Let them do the arithmetic. At $149/month, 15 cancellations/month = $2,235 out the door, every month. If the system recovers 4 of those 15 cancellations, it pays for itself in year one from the retained revenue alone — not counting the no-show reduction, the PT renewal sequence, or the lapsed member campaigns.

The fitness studio owner who understands that math is not buying a technology expense. They're buying a retention system that pays for itself. That framing is the close.
