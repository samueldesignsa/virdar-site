## Target 1: Auto Repair Shop — Hurst or Bedford

**What they do:** Independent auto repair. Not a dealership, not a Firestone or Midas franchise. Owner-run shop, 3–8 bays, general repair and maintenance (brakes, oil changes, tires, diagnostics). Local reputation built over years of word-of-mouth. Revenue $400,000–$900,000/year depending on size and specialization.

**Where to look:** Google Maps — "auto repair Hurst TX" or "mechanic Bedford TX." Look for local branding (owner name or area name in the shop name), 50–200 Google reviews, in business 8+ years. Avoid franchise chains. The Pipeline Rd corridor in Hurst and the Bedford Rd / Brown Trail area in Bedford have clusters of independent shops.

**What to notice before walking in:**
- How recent is their most recent Google review? A shop with 120 reviews and the last one from four months ago has a visible review generation gap.
- Do they have a way to book an appointment online, or does it require a phone call? A shop that takes appointments by phone only is losing the customer who searches at 10pm and wants to schedule for Tuesday.
- Are there any 1-star or 2-star reviews in the last 6 months with no response from the owner? Non-response on negative reviews is a trust signal issue in a market where reputation is everything.

**Hook:** "Auto shops I work with in this area have one problem that costs them the most money: the customer who came in for an oil change, the tech found the brake pads were at 20%, the advisor mentioned it, the customer said 'not today' — and nobody called that customer back in 30 days. How often does a declined service actually get a follow-up? And what does that follow-up look like right now — text, call, nothing?"

**Revenue math:** A shop doing 25 oil changes per day has technicians noticing declined services on every third vehicle — brakes, filters, fluids, tires. If the shop logs 15 declined services per week and follows up with 0% of them (common), versus 40% with an automated text at 21 days ("We noticed last visit your brake pads were at 20% — they're typically ready for service at that level. Want us to schedule you in?"), at an average declined service value of $320, that's $1,920/week — $100,000/year — in deferred revenue recovered from customers who were already coming to the shop.

**What to notice before walking in:**

- Review response rate: Count the reviews and count the responses. A practice with 180 reviews and 8 responses has a visible gap. Patients considering switching dentists read reviews and they read responses — a non-responsive practice looks less attentive than the one down the street.

**Hook:** "Dental practices in this area have a recall gap that's very consistent: the patient due for a cleaning in March who got a postcard, didn't call back, and is now 90 days overdue. The offices that reach out specifically — 'we noticed you missed your March appointment, we have a Tuesday at 10am, does that work?' — recover 60–70% of those patients. **Revenue math:** A Bedford dental practice with 750 active patients improving return rate from 70% to 80% adds 75 hygiene visits per year. At $185/visit, that's $13,875 in direct recall revenue. **What they do:** Independent hair salon or barbershop. Owner-operated. Not a Great Clips or Sport Clips franchise. 2–6 chairs. Local clientele, mostly appointment-based, some walk-ins.

The Hurst Blvd and Pipeline Rd areas and the Bedford Rd corridor have independent salons that have been in the same spot for years.

**What to notice before walking in:**
- Online booking: Is there a booking link on their Google profile? Many HEB salons still take appointments by phone or walk-in only. This isn't a fatal flaw for the existing clientele, but it's a barrier for new clients who search and want to book immediately.
- No-show problem: This is the most expensive problem in the industry. A 3-chair salon with 3 no-shows per day at $65 average ticket is losing $195/day — $50,000+/year in confirmed appointments that walk out as gaps on the schedule.
- Rebooking rate: After a haircut or color service, most clients leave without scheduling their next appointment. The ones who do rebook are the ones who remembered to ask or were asked. The ones who don't rebook come back when they remember — which is when they're already past due and looking at competitors on Google.

**Hook:** "The gap that costs salons the most money isn't slow days — it's no-shows on booked appointments and clients who leave without rebooking. A confirmed appointment two days out with no reminder is a 20–30% no-show rate. With a text reminder the morning of — 'See you at 2pm today, Sarah!' — that drops to 5–8%. How many no-shows are you seeing in a typical week? And when a client leaves after a great service, how often does your team ask them to book their next one before they walk out?"

**Revenue math:** A 3-chair salon with 60 appointments per week reducing no-shows from 4 per day to 1 per day recovers 15 appointments per week. At $65 average ticket, that's $975/week — $50,700/year — in recovered revenue from appointments that were already confirmed. Systematic rebooking adds to that: a salon where 35% of clients rebook before leaving versus 15% adds 12 retained appointments per week to the forward schedule.
