### Why this zone today

Independent optometry is one of the strongest automation targets in the DFW Metroplex right now. Southlake, Colleyville, and Grapevine are high-income suburban markets where patients have premium vision insurance — VSP, EyeMed, Humana Vision — and above-average frame and lens spend. An independent OD in these zip codes is seeing patients who spend $400 to $800 per visit on average.

The competitive pressure on independent optometrists is real. LensCrafters, Warby Parker, Target Optical, and Costco Optical are all within driving distance of every patient in this zone. Patients who don't hear from their independent OD will eventually wander. The practices that keep patients coming back aren't doing it by being cheaper — they're doing it by communicating better and more consistently than the chains can.

## Target 1: Vision Source Southlake / FM 1709 Corridor

**Find it:** Search "optometrist Southlake TX" on Google Maps. Look for practices branded as "Vision Source [Southlake]" or independently named practices along FM 1709 (Southlake Blvd), SH 114, or the Southlake Town Square area. Vision Source is a cooperative — each location is independently owned by the OD. Not a chain.

**What they do:** Full-scope optometry: comprehensive eye exams, contact lens fittings, medical eye care. Frame and lens dispensary on-site. Typically 1 to 2 doctors, 3 to 5 support staff. Active patient base: 800 to 2,000 patients depending on how long the practice has been open.

**What I noticed:** Vision Source practices are typically mid-size independent practices with a real EHR (Eyefinity is the dominant one in this network). They have the data infrastructure to support automation — recall dates, contact lens records, insurance information — but the EHR's built-in recall tools are generic. The automated benefit expiration campaign (October–December targeting calendar-year plan patients) is the clearest gap because the generic recall module sends every patient the same message on the same schedule regardless of insurance type.

**Best hook:** "Your recall module is probably sending everyone the same message — but your VSP patients are different from your EyeMed patients, and both of those are different from your patients without vision insurance. What does your October look like right now, and are you filling it deliberately or waiting to see what comes in?" Any practice owner who's ever had an empty chair in November when patients still had unused benefits will know exactly what you mean.

**Leave-behind:** `virdar.co/blog/ai-automation-for-optometry-practices.html` — quantifies the benefit expiration campaign specifically.

## Target 2: Independent OD in Colleyville (Glade Road / Precinct Line Corridor)

**Find it:** Search "eye doctor Colleyville TX" or "optometrist Colleyville TX" on Google Maps. The Glade Road and Precinct Line Road area in Colleyville has professional services clusters. Filter for independently branded practices, not retail chains.

**What they do:** Typically an owner-OD with 10+ years in the market, strong personal lines patient relationships. Colleyville patient demographic skews older than Southlake — more presbyopia, more progressive lens prescriptions, more medical eye care (dry eye, glaucoma monitoring). Average revenue per patient visit is higher because lens complexity is higher.

**What I noticed:** Established independent ODs in Colleyville often have a large "lapsed" patient database — patients who were seen 2 to 4 years ago and have since drifted without anyone doing anything about it. These practices have been open long enough to accumulate 2,000 to 4,000 patients in the system but may only be actively seeing 800 to 1,200 per year. The gap between the total database and the active patient count is the automation opportunity.

**Best hook:** "How many patients do you have in your system total versus how many came in last year? Most practices I talk to have a 40 to 50 percent gap — patients who saw them once or twice and then just stopped coming back without anyone reaching out. What does your lapsed patient reactivation look like?" Let them answer. If the answer is "we don't really do much" or "we send a card every year," the reactivation sequence story is the pitch.

**Qualifier question:** "What practice management system are you on?" Eyefinity, Revolution EHR, Crystal PM, and Compulink are all integrable. Ask before going deep.

## Target 3: Grapevine Eye Care (Main Street or William D Tate Ave Area)

**Find it:** Search "optometrist Grapevine TX" on Google Maps. The William D Tate Ave and Grapevine Mills area, as well as the historic Main Street corridor, have independent optometry practices. Look for owner-operated practices — the decision-maker will be in the building.

**What they do:** Personal lines eye care with strong community roots. Grapevine has a long-established residential community, so practices here often have multigenerational patient relationships. Families who've been patients for 10+ years. The OD is often the same person who saw the parents and is now seeing the kids.

**What I noticed:** Family practices in Grapevine are walk-in gold because the owner-OD is typically at the desk between patients and is highly accessible. The contact lens reorder automation story lands particularly well here — a practice with a large young adult patient base (college-age kids who grew up as patients) loses a disproportionate amount of contact lens revenue to 1-800-Contacts. The practice already has the relationship. The reorder just never gets prompted.

**Best hook:** "What percentage of your contact lens patients reorder through you versus ordering online? I ask because most independent practices I've talked to know they're losing reorders to 1-800-Contacts, but nobody's done anything systematic about it." The reorder recovery math is simple: 400 active contact lens patients, 30% ordering online instead of through the practice = 120 reorders per year you're not getting. At $220 average net, that's $26,400 walking out the door annually.

## Target 4: Eye Doctors on Southlake Blvd / Carroll Ave (Southlake Town Square Adjacent)

**Find it:** Search "eye exam Southlake TX" on Google Maps and look for practices in the Town Square area or the Carroll Avenue corridor south of SH 114. These practices benefit from foot traffic proximity and are often in professional office parks.

**What they do:** Typically a 2-doctor practice with a strong optical dispensary. High-income Southlake patient demographic means above-average frame spend — $600 to $1,200 per frame transaction is not unusual in this zip code. The optical dispensary is a significant revenue driver alongside the exam revenue.

**What I noticed:** In high-income areas, the frame upgrade cycle is the underworked automation opportunity. A patient who spent $900 on progressive lenses in 2024 has $200+ in frame benefit available again in 2026. Most practices don't reach out proactively — they wait for the patient to come back when they want to. An automated frame upgrade sequence targeting patients at 22–24 months post-purchase, with a message referencing their insurance benefit reset, has a measurably higher conversion rate than waiting.

**Best hook:** "For a patient who came in two years ago and got progressive lenses — what does your outreach look like for that patient now that their frame benefit has reset? Are you reaching out proactively or waiting for them to call?" Most practices wait. That's the gap.

## Target 5: Keller or North Richland Hills Independent OD (Adjacent Zone)

**Find it:** Search "optometrist Keller TX" or "eye doctor North Richland Hills TX" on Google Maps. The Keller and North Richland Hills market is just south and east of the primary zone — strong residential base, many independent ODs who serve the suburban mid-income demographic.

**What they do:** Typically solo or 2-doctor practice focused on personal lines eye care. Slightly different patient mix than Southlake — more middle-income families, higher volume of pediatric exams, contact lens patients skew toward younger demographics.

**What I noticed:** Higher-volume practices in Keller/NRH have a no-show problem that's more acute than lower-volume practices. If you're scheduling 35+ patients per day, a 15% no-show rate is 5 empty slots per day. The waitlist filling automation — where a cancellation triggers automatic outreach to waitlisted patients — is the specific pitch here. "How many empty slots did you have last month from same-day cancellations? And do you have a waitlist system that fills those automatically, or is it manual?"

**Best approach:** This is more of a volume pitch than an affluent-patient pitch. Less about frame revenue, more about appointment utilization. The ROI math is per-exam rather than per-transaction.

## Target 6: Trophy Club / Westlake Area Vision Practice

**Find it:** Search "optometrist Trophy Club TX" or "eye doctor Westlake TX" on Google Maps. This is the western edge of the walk-in zone. Lower density of practices, but very high-income patient demographic — Westlake is one of the most affluent zip codes in the entire DFW area.

**What they do:** Typically a small independent practice serving the Trophy Club and Westlake residential communities. High average transaction value. Patient base is smaller but extremely high value — a practice here might see only 15 patients per day but average $600+ per visit.

**What I noticed:** In very small, high-income practices, the referral automation story is the strongest hook. These practices grow primarily by word of mouth — the community is tight enough that one strong patient advocate is worth dozens of recall campaigns. An automated post-visit referral ask, timed to 24 hours after a frame pickup, systematically captures the referral opportunity that's currently happening only when the OD thinks to ask. "If you asked every happy patient for a referral at exactly the right moment, how many more new patients do you think you'd get? And are you doing that consistently right now?"

## Logistics for today

**Best time to walk in:** 10am–noon. Optometry practices have morning appointment blocks that run 8 to 11am. The window between 10am and noon is when the OD or office manager is between patients and accessible.

**What to wear:** Business casual. Optometry is a healthcare-adjacent professional environment. Match the clinical-professional vibe.

**Leave-behind for every stop:** `virdar.co/blog/ai-automation-for-optometry-practices.html` — published this morning, written specifically for independent eye care practices.

**Opening for every stop:** "I work with independent optometry practices on their patient recall and reorder systems — automated sequences for annual exams, contact lens reorders, and benefit expiration campaigns. Do you have five minutes?"

**Qualifying question before going deep:** "What practice management system are you on?" This tells you whether the build is straightforward (Eyefinity, Revolution EHR, Crystal PM, Compulink) or needs more investigation.

**Three strongest hooks by scenario:**
- **Practice with 1,500+ patients but only 700 seen last year:** Lapsed patient reactivation. "The patients are in your system. They're not gone. They just haven't heard from you."
- **Practice losing contact lens reorders to online:** Reorder automation + prescription expiration tie-in. "$26,000 a year in contact lens revenue going to 1-800-Contacts isn't a pricing problem — it's a timing problem."
- **Practice with empty Q4 schedule:** Benefit expiration campaign. "Your VSP patients' frame allowances expire December 31. Do they know that? Do they know in October, when there's still time to schedule?"

**Close:** "I'll send you a one-page breakdown showing what this would return for a practice your size — what the recall system does, what the contact lens sequence does, what the benefit expiration campaign does in Q4, and what the whole thing costs to build. If the math makes sense, we can do a 30-minute call." Then Calendly.
