## Targets

### 1. Independent Med Spa on McKinney Ave (Uptown corridor)
- **Address:** McKinney Ave between Lemmon Ave and Hall St — there are several independent aesthetic clinics along this stretch. Search "med spa McKinney Ave Dallas" on Google Maps before going. Look for independently branded practices (not DFW Medspa chain locations), with 50 to 300 Google reviews.
- **What they do:** Injectables (Botox, fillers), laser treatments, skin care services. High-ticket, appointment-dependent. Average service $300–$800.
- **What to notice before walking in:** Check their Google Maps listing — do they have online booking, or does it say "call to book"? Call-to-book practices are the prime target. Also check their review count and the dates of recent reviews — a practice with 80 reviews but none in the past 90 days has a review request problem.
- **Best hook:** No-show reduction and rebooking sequences. "What's your no-show rate right now, and what do you do with clients who haven't booked in 90 days?"
- **Conversation opener:** "I build confirmation and rebooking systems for med spas. A practice doing 25 to 30 appointments a week with a 10% no-show rate is losing $8,000 to $12,000 a month in already-booked revenue. I build the system that cuts that rate by 60 to 70 percent and automatically re-engages lapsed clients. I have a piece up today specifically about this — do you have 5 minutes?"

### 2. Milk + Honey Spa (or similar independent day spa)
- **Address:** Uptown / West Village area. Search "day spa Uptown Dallas" — look for independently owned day spas offering facials, massage, and body treatments. Not a chain franchise.
- **What they do:** Full-service spa with appointment-dependent revenue. Average ticket $120–$300. Strong client retention and rebooking dependency.
- **What to notice:** Look at their booking experience. Most independent day spas use a booking link that routes through MindBody, Vagaro, or Square — often with no automated confirmation or reminder set up beyond a basic email. Call the number on their Google listing mid-morning and see how fast they answer. That's a data point.
- **Best hook:** Lapsed client reactivation and recurring appointment reminders. "How many clients do you have in your system who came in once or twice and haven't been back in 6 months?"
- **Conversation opener:** "I work with day spas and wellness businesses on the retention side. Most spas have 200 to 400 clients in their database who came in and haven't been back — not because they had a bad experience, but because nobody reached back out. I build the system that does that automatically. What does your rebooking follow-up look like right now?"

### 3. Independent Blowout Bar or Hair Salon on Knox-Henderson
- **Address:** Knox St between Travis and Central Expressway, or Henderson Ave. This strip has several independent salons and blowout bars. Look for independently owned (not a DryBar franchise), with strong Google presence and 100+ reviews — that means established clientele worth retaining.
- **What they do:** Appointment-based hair services. Average ticket $60–$200. High repeat-client dependency. Major revenue leak: clients who come monthly for 6 months, then fall off.
- **What to notice:** Do they have an online booking link? If it points to StyleSeat or Square, ask whether they have automated reminders set up — most don't, or set them up once and forgot about it. Also look at how they respond to Google reviews. A salon with 150 reviews and only 20% owner responses has a reputation management gap.
- **Best hook:** Recurring appointment reminders for regular clients. "When a client who used to come every 4 to 6 weeks hasn't booked in 10 weeks — what happens? Does your system flag that, or does it just sit there?"
- **Conversation opener:** "I build retention systems for salons. The clients who drift off aren't gone — they're just not being reminded. A message that goes out automatically at 8 or 10 weeks since their last visit, with a direct booking link, brings back 15 to 25 percent of lapsed regulars. What does your outreach look like for clients who haven't booked in a while?"

### 4. Boutique Pilates or Barre Studio (Uptown / Knox-Henderson)
- **Address:** Search "pilates studio Uptown Dallas" or "barre studio Knox Henderson Dallas" on Google Maps. Look for independently owned studios — not a Club Pilates franchise. The independently operated ones have a real owner who makes decisions, unlike franchise locations where pricing and systems are centralized.
- **What they do:** Small group fitness classes, appointment-dependent, high membership and class-pack model. Major pain points: class no-shows (they still pay for the instructor), membership churn, and people who bought a 10-class pack and only used 3.
- **What to notice:** Check their website — do they have a waitlist feature for full classes? Most use MindBody or Pike13. Look at their Instagram (usually linked from their Google listing) — are they posting regularly, and are they getting engagement? Suggests an owner who's active and marketing-minded.
- **Best hook:** Class attendance, unused class pack follow-up, and membership renewal. "What happens when someone buys a 10-class pack and uses 3 of them — does your system reach out, or do they just expire?"
- **Conversation opener:** "I build follow-up systems for boutique fitness studios. The clients who buy a class pack and drop off are your best reactivation targets — they already paid, they already decided they liked it, they just got busy. An automated sequence that checks in at the 30-day and 60-day marks recovers a real percentage of those. What does your outreach look like for lapsed pack holders?"

### 5. Independent Dermatology or Medical Aesthetics Practice (Oak Lawn / Turtle Creek)
- **Address:** Search "dermatology Oak Lawn Dallas" or "aesthetic medicine Turtle Creek Dallas." Look for small independent practices with 1 to 3 providers — not large hospital-affiliated dermatology groups. The independent owner is the decision-maker and has full control over operations.
- **What they do:** Medical-grade aesthetics, skin care, cosmetic dermatology. Average ticket $400–$2,000. High compliance with follow-up appointments is critical — incomplete treatment series means worse outcomes and unhappy clients.
- **What to notice:** Call the office mid-morning. How fast do they answer? How professional is the hold message or voicemail? If they don't answer on a Monday morning, they have a phone coverage problem. Check Google reviews — a practice with 60+ reviews and a 4.7+ rating is worth targeting. One with a lot of reviews complaining about appointment reminders or scheduling has an obvious pain point.
- **Best hook:** Treatment series compliance and follow-up reminders. "For a patient doing a laser series — what's your system for making sure they come back for all 6 sessions on schedule?"
- **Conversation opener:** "I work with aesthetic medical practices on the patient follow-up side. For practices doing laser series or multi-session treatments, the difference between a patient completing all 6 sessions and dropping off at 3 is often just whether they got the right reminder at the right time. I build the system that handles that automatically. Do you have 5 minutes?"
