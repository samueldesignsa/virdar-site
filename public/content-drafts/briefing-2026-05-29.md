## One Insight

There's a pattern across the last four zones (Southlake, Lewisville/Flower Mound, and now Keller) that's worth naming: high-income suburban service businesses have a specific kind of resistance to the pitch.

It's not skepticism about AI or automation. It's skepticism about whether the problem is big enough to justify the change. A Keller dentist with 220 reviews and a busy schedule thinks the practice is running fine. A Keller electrician with 80 jobs booked thinks the estimate close rate is what it is.

The pivot that works in these zones: don't open with the problem. Open with a number. "At your estimate volume, a 10-point improvement in close rate is worth $86,000/year" lands differently than "you have a follow-up problem." The second framing requires them to believe there's a problem. The first framing is just math — they can check it against their own numbers in 30 seconds.

In lower-income zones (HEB, Grand Prairie, Lewisville Main St), business owners feel the pain more acutely and respond to problem-framing. In Keller, they're doing well enough that the pain isn't obvious — start with the math, let them work backward to the problem.
