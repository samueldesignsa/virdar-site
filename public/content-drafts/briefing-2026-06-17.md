## One Insight

Yesterday's Park Cities experience is worth contrasting with today's Lakewood zone. In Park Cities, Beverly was walking into 20-year practices with sophisticated owners, high skepticism, and large revenue at stake. The pitch required extreme specificity to break through that skepticism — specific school district start dates, specific dollar figures for their specific practice size.

Today's Lakewood / East Dallas market is different in an important way: these owners are less skeptical, but they're also less likely to have been tracking their own loss numbers. A Park Cities orthodontist might push back on Beverly's estimate of "35 unconverted consults" because he knows his own numbers. A Lakewood chiro practice owner may not know how many patients finished active care in Q1 and haven't been reached since. That's not a weakness — it's an opportunity. The first question is often the revelation: "How many patients do you have who came in 14 months ago and haven't rebooked?" They don't know. Once they look it up, they see the problem.

In Lakewood, Beverly's pitch is most effective when it moves from question to calculation in the same conversation. Ask the number. They look it up or estimate it. Beverly does the math in front of them. The math becomes the close, not the technology demo. That's true everywhere Beverly has worked this spring — but it's especially true in a market where the business owner is running the practice and hasn't stopped to count what's sitting idle.
