## One Insight

The HEB zone is a market test for a different buyer archetype.

Southlake buyers respond to premium revenue math — the $47,500/year in managed fees a financial advisor loses to poor communication, the $168,000 in patient lifetime value a dental practice preserves through systematic recall. The numbers land because the account sizes are large and the ROI conversation is easy.

HEB buyers are different. They've been pitched before. They're skeptical. They respond better to the specific, observable problem: "I know what's leaking at your shop, and I know what it's costing you per week." The declined service follow-up for an auto shop is a perfect example — it's a gap that any shop owner can immediately verify by looking at their own service records. "How many declined services did you log last week? How many got a follow-up call?" The answer is almost always "I don't know" and "not many." That's the opening.

In HEB, the math comes second. The observable, verifiable problem description comes first. If Beverly walks in and says "I know what your declined service follow-up rate is" before the owner has a chance to object, they have to engage with whether she's right — and she almost certainly is.
